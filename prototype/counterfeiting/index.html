(Drug) Counterfeiting

Grace Woo, June 2012

Motivation

I didn't think that I would be a part of any counterfeiting initiatives when working on VRCodes (my PhD thesis work) in the Viral Spaces group. The work, however, has attracted parallels in areas of fraud protection as well as counterfeit prevention

First, I'll preface by saying that the camera-based counterfeit prevention techniques that I'm about to describe are not the holy grail for fighting counterfeiting. The only fool-proof way to test whether or not something is "real" is to make a mass spectrogram test (see Bove et al). In other words, specifically for the problem of detecting drug counterfeiting, it doesn't matter at the end of the day whether it's a real brand or a fake brand. The important thing is to verify whether what you are consuming is what you think you are consuming.

Challenge

However, when travelling in a third-world country and presented with two pill bottles that look exactly identical, it is still not possible to pull out the handheld mass spectrometer to do a quick test (yet). As a result, many are thinking about intermediate solutions like cryptographic labels which can be verified with a phone call or text message. mPedigree, orange SMS, and Pharmasecure are a few examples of text-message verification systems.

In fact, there are plenty of intermediary solutions that work with various levels of security. Watermarks are labels on print substrate that are difficult for the human eye to discern but can be picked up by a camera. RFIDs can be deployed and verified with a handheld RFID reader.

These intermediate solutions all boil down to three components. First, it's important to have a good frontend. Frontend refers to the RFID itself, the visual mark or the text-based input sequence. It's a lot like printing good money. You have to make it so that it's not obvious for a human to distinguish between a real piece of cash and a fake piece of cash. Then, you have to have a camera be able to distinguish small perturbations that set the real mark apart from the faked mark. All of those little details that get printed on money serve the purpose of making it difficult to be copied by the adversary. Hence, comparing along this dimension implies that RFID is a better solution that a visual mark. Unfortunately, as we all know via common sense, with enough determination nearly any tag which does not embed a secure code can be copied.

The challenge in creating a tag for counterfeit protection is that it's not enough to make a physical tag that is hard to copy. Second, you must also devise a method which has enough digital capacity to carry a cryptographic key. This little bit of data is crucial. Thie piece of data should uniquely identify the thing that is being consumed. Just like those annoying one-time use Windows keys that you have into your "nicely-acquired" software, each object needs to have a unique key (at the minimum) which allows the packaging to be tallied in a centralized and verifying database when it is scanned.

That is still not enough. Third, you must create a trusted network for verification that is not entirely centralized. Just like money, the strength and participation of the users of money helps verify whether or not the codes in circulation are valid. Backtrack from that and it becomes obvious that you want as many eyes as possible checking out the validity of the scanned or swiped code.

These trusted networks can be created in a variety of ways using as much gatherable information as possible. For example, if there are a few "invalid" codes that are captured in a particular location, there's a high chance that everything else there is false as well. That's just one way of creating validation. Other ways include reputation systems, social graphs and specialized verifiers.

Going back to my own thesis work, VRCodes an example of active visual codes which can embed digital information that takes advantage of how the camera sees something different from what we see. For example, what may just look like a gray surface to our eyes can actually embed a lot of data underneath. When applied to counterfeit protection, many of the same techniques can be used to create a "tag" that appears indistinguishable to our eyes but actually carry cryptographic information underneath.

In other words, VRCodes can help hit two out of the three important points. When VRCodes are used with a verification backend, the solution as a whole can be used to combat counterfeiting using the existing hardware that are in people's hands.

It's not easy. Even when a proposed solution fits all of the criteria from a technical standpoint, one must make sure that the right parties are using those tags. For example, it's not clear that the drug companies with the most amount of money to invest in these tags/verification networks are the same ones that have the best drugs. What happens to the non-name brands and can they still be encouraged to create good drugs with an intermediate verification system?

The ultimate goal is to provide the highest quality product to the right people regardless of prices and brand-recognition. The best way to do that is empowering the user with a true physical fact-checker (see universal tricorder).
